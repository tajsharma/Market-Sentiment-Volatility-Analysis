The starting question: What type of news moves the market?

Branches: 
    1. What happened? 
    2. When it happened?
    3. Who was involved?
    4. How it was reported?
    5. When it happened?
    6. How significant was it perceived?

Hypotheses:
    1. What happened? (Market_event)
        a. I believe that will policy/regalatory events will cause higher 
        volaltility in market events becaus most traders takes these into
        consideration as these events directly impact how companies can 
        operate and it influences how they import/export services/goods. 

        b. Macro-economic indicators move slowly and are often priced in 
        before official releases, so the actual announcement may cause 
        less surprise and therefore less volatility.

    2. Where it happened? (Sector)
        a. I believe we will find that sectors will show clearly different
        levels of volaltility as sectors like Tech (reliance on future earnings, 
        rapid innovation, new players), Energy(gas/oil prices going up and down),
        Materials (manufacturing laws, supply chain), and Pharmaceuticals (depends 
        on patents and approvals) will be a lot mlore volatile than Consumer Goods 
        which have a constant demand, Utilities which are essential have lower 
        price fluctuations and Real Estate which is tangible and has long term
        value. 

        b. The spread between the most and least volatile sectors will be under 
        0.5 percentage points because the news events in this dataset are 
        macro-level in nature — they affect entire markets, not individual 
        industries. Sector-specific sensitivity exists but is muted by the 
        broad scope of these events.
    
    3. Who was involved? (Related_Company)
        a. Financial institutions like JP Morgan will show disproportionately higher 
        volatility during Central Bank Meetings and Interest Rate Changes compared 
        to their overall average, while tech companies like Tesla or Apple will spike 
        more during Regulatory Changes and Trade Tariff Announcements — because each 
        company's core business model is exposed to different policy levers.

        b. Companies that appear more frequently in the dataset will show lower average 
        volatility per event because frequent coverage suggests routine news flow, 
        while companies that appear rarely are only covered during exceptional events 
        which tend to be more disruptive.

    4. How was it reported? (Sentiment)
        a. I believe that negative sentiment will drive more volatility because humans
        are more risk averse and would be more likely to sell a stock because 'the 
        internet will not be needed' for example rather than buy a new stock that 
        promises ' a lot of growth' Negative news sticks more to us rather than good news. 
        
        b. ews source will have minimal independent effect on volatility because all 
        outlets are reporting on the same underlying events. Any differences in volatility
        by source will disappear once you control for event type — meaning the source's 
        apparent effect is actually just a reflection of which events they choose to cover.

    5. When it happened? 
        a. Mondays will show higher average volatility than other weekdays because news 
        accumulates over the weekend and hits the market all at once. Fridays will show the 
        second-highest volatility as traders rebalance positions before the weekend to reduce 
        risk exposure.

        b. Volatility will not be evenly distributed across the dataset's timeline — it will 
        cluster into 'regimes' where several weeks of elevated volatility are followed by 
        calm periods. These clusters will coincide with periods where multiple high-impact 
        event types occurred simultaneously.

        c. Months that coincide with corporate earnings season (January, April, July, October) 
        will show elevated volatility because earnings reports flood the market with new 
        information simultaneously, forcing rapid repricing across sectors.

    6. How significant was it perceived? (Market_index and Impact_level)
        a. Events labeled 'High' impact will show at least 50% more average volatility than 
        'Low' impact events. If this relationship is weak, it suggests the Impact_Level label
        in the dataset is poorly assigned and not a reliable feature.

        b. Different indices will show different baseline volatility levels. A tech-heavy index
        like NASDAQ will show higher volatility on Technology and IPO events, while a broad index 
        like the S&P 500 will react more evenly across event types because it's diversified.

        c. High impact + negative sentiment events will show disproportionately higher volatility
        than what you'd expect from adding the two effects separately. In other words, the combination
        is worse than the sum of its parts.
